A Brief History of the Interlocking Puzzle

The concept of pieces that hold each other together through their own geometry—without nails or glue—is one of the oldest forms of entertainment. While many associate these puzzles with the 19th-century European toy boom, their roots stretch back much further.

The Lu Ban Lock

Ancient Chinese architecture utilized complex wooden joints that allowed massive temples to stand for centuries. Legend has it that the master carpenter Lu Ban (507–444 BC) created the first interlocking puzzle based on these architectural principles to test his students' spatial reasoning.

"To solve a puzzle is to understand the mind of the architect who built it."

By the 1700s, these 'burr' puzzles had traveled to Europe, where they became a staple of the educational curriculum for young engineers and architects. Today, we continue this lineage by combining ancient joinery logic with modern precision technology.
